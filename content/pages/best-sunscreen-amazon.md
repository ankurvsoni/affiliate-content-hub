> **Affiliate disclosure:** This page includes affiliate links. If you buy through them, we may earn a commission at no extra cost to you.

## Quick verdict

SPF 30 is the minimum; SPF 50 is better for extended outdoor time. The real differentiator is formula: mineral sunscreens (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) sit on the skin and are gentler for sensitive skin; chemical sunscreens absorb UV and tend to feel lighter. For the face, finish matters as much as protection — a sunscreen you'll skip because it feels greasy defeats the purpose.

## Who should skip this

If you're buying sunscreen for occasional beach trips only, any drugstore SPF 50 does the job — you don't need to optimize online. Skip the premium options unless you're wearing sunscreen daily or have specific skin concerns.

## Common mistakes

- Buying SPF 15 thinking it's "enough for daily use" — the AAD recommends minimum SPF 30
- Not reapplying after 2 hours outdoors, regardless of SPF rating
- Choosing a formula based on SPF number alone while ignoring texture (and then not wearing it consistently)

## FAQ

### What's the difference between SPF 50 and SPF 100?
SPF 50 blocks about 98% of UVB rays; SPF 100 blocks about 99%. In practice, the difference is small — proper application and reapplication matter far more than chasing a higher number.

### Is mineral or chemical sunscreen better?
Neither is universally better. Mineral is gentler on sensitive skin and better for reef ecosystems. Chemical feels lighter and is easier to apply evenly. Use what you'll actually wear every day.

### Does sunscreen expire?
Yes. Most sunscreens have a 3-year shelf life. Expired sunscreen degrades and won't protect effectively — check the date on the bottom.
